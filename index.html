58 hectares blending French formal, English landscape, and Japanese strolling gardens. Over 1,000 cherry trees across 65 varieties — late March is Somei Yoshino peak. Enter Shinjuku Gate → left into formal French lawns → arc right through the massive English meadow (gentle hill to roll down) → Japanese traditional section with koi pond and Taiwan Pavilion → end at the tropical greenhouse with banana trees, carnivorous plants, and giant cycads.
Late March magic: On windy days petals fall like pink snow across the lawns. Stop walking when it happens — it's one of those moments. Admission ¥500 adults, kids under 15 free.
The enormous English meadow is pure energy-burning territory after travel days. The gentle hill in the center = rolling. The koi ponds near the traditional garden section almost always have turtles basking on rocks that kids find every time.
Animal spotting list before you enter: Koi in 5+ colors, wild turtles, egrets fishing the shallows, pigeons so tame they land on your arm. Whoever spots all 4 first wins a crepe in Harajuku.
Greenhouse creature hunt: Inside the tropical greenhouse: carnivorous plants (sundews, pitcher plants), giant cycads over 200 years old, tropical insects. Free with admission. Kids who love weird biology go feral.
Hello Kitty side quest note: Sanrio Puroland (full Hello Kitty theme park) is 45 min from Shinjuku by Keio Line. Not on this itinerary but a half-day option if demand is high. Miracle Gift Parade and Sanrio Boat Ride are the highlights.
Coffee before
All Seasons Coffee — 5 min from Shinjuku-gyoemmae. Specialty café with live bean roasting. Pudding affogato (espresso over caramel-scorched custard), excellent lattes. Get coffees to go and carry into the garden.
Inside snacks
No food inside. Grab from 7-Eleven near the gate: onigiri, melon bread, Calpico drinks (milky carbonated — kids universally love), Pocky. Stock up before entering.
Best family photo: The Taiwan Pavilion reflected in the pond with cherry blossoms overhead — southern end of the traditional garden section. Most tourists walk past it.
Hanami tip: Locals spread blue tarps and picnic under the blossoms — completely normal. Grab convenience store food and find a spot on the main lawn. No reservations. One of the most Japanese things you can do in Tokyo.

The Shrine

The most important Shinto shrine in Tokyo, set in a 70-hectare forest of 120,000 donated trees. The gravel approach from the Harajuku Gate feels like a decompression chamber — urban noise falls away instantly. The 12-meter torii gate near the entrance is from a single 1,500-year-old cypress. At the main courtyard: two bows, two claps, one bow (the Shinto greeting). Traditional wedding processions cross daily 11am–1pm.
Don't miss: A wall of sake barrels (kazaridaru) at the entrance sits next to a wall of Burgundy wine barrels from France — a gift symbolizing the cultural exchange Emperor Meiji championed. Great photo, great story for kids.
Omikuji fortune slips: ¥100 — shake the numbered stick, find the matching drawer, pull your fortune. Bad luck? Tie it to the rack and leave it behind. Do one per person. Compare fortunes over lunch.
Ema wish plaques: Wooden plaques (¥500–1,000) where you write a wish and hang it. Kids take this seriously. Let each one write theirs privately.
Animal carving hunt: How many different animals can they find on the shrine buildings? Dragons, phoenixes, cranes hidden throughout. The crunching gravel underfoot makes even restless kids go quiet.
Coffee & Matcha after the shrine — pick one
Fuglen Tokyo ⭐7 min walk south into quiet Tomigaya. The Norwegian café that sparked Tokyo's third-wave coffee scene in 2012. Scandinavian-Japanese minimalism, vintage mid-century furniture. Oat milk available. ¥700–900. Open daily from 7am. Our top pick for this stop.
Little Nap Coffee StandA tiny cult-status coffee stand tucked on the western edge of Yoyogi Park. No indoor seating — stand, sip, and watch locals cycle past. Cappuccino and long black are exceptional. Perfect grab-and-go before the Yoyogi picnic. ¥600–750. Open daily 9am.
Matcha Stand Maruni (Tomigaya)Minimal interior, ceremonial-grade Uji matcha prepared to order in front of you. Single and double shot options, oat and almond milk. The matcha soft serve is one of the best in the city. Great for kids who want something sweet. ¥700–900.
How to choose: Serious coffee → Fuglen. Quick grab on the way to the park → Little Nap. Matcha for kids (or adults) → Matcha Stand Maruni. All three are within a 10-min walk of the shrine's south gate.

The world's busiest pedestrian intersection — up to 3,000 people cross simultaneously from every angle. Cross at street level at least twice (once with flow, once diagonal). The Meguro River runs through Nakameguro, lined on both banks by cherry trees that in late March create a canopy of blossoms. At night they're strung with small lanterns — yozakura (night cherry blossom viewing) is a genuine Tokyo tradition, and Nakameguro is the best place to experience it. Intimate, stylish, residential.
Late March specifics: Lanterns go up along the canal ~March 20–April 5 during peak bloom. Walking the canal at dusk with cherry blossoms overhead and lanterns glowing is the best possible end to Day 1.
Onibus Coffee — tiny wooden cabin right next to the canal and train tracks. Single-origin pour-overs, excellent banana bread. Reviewer notes it's "practically next to a playground" — great for parents.
Starbucks Reserve Roastery: The famous Nakameguro location is partially below street level with floor-to-ceiling windows facing the cherry blossom canal. Even without buying, walk past it — architecturally stunning. During blossom season the queue moves. Worth seeing once.

A dense grid of narrow lanes with fishmongers, egg vendors, professional knife shops, and tiny restaurants doing one or two things with extraordinary precision. Walk a full loop first without buying to survey everything, then go back for what you want.
Family of 6 strategy: Split into two roving groups with a meetup at 11:30am. Cover more ground, avoid single-file bottlenecks in the narrow lanes.
Tamagoyaki ⭐Rolled egg on a wooden skewer — custard-thick, slightly sweet, fresh off the pan. Marutake or Yamachō. 5–10 min line. ¥400. Most kid-friendly food at the market.
Vegetable tempura skewersSeasonal veg fried to order — sweet potato, lotus root, shishito peppers, eggplant. Light, greaseless batter, crispy and hot. Kids who don't usually like vegetables often convert here. ¥200–400 per skewer.
Tuna sashimiFresh-cut from the tuna stalls. At this quality and price (¥600–1,200), you won't find better outside a high-end restaurant.
Japanese strawberries ⭐Peak season is late March. Intensely sweet, enormous. A flat runs ¥800–1,500. Don't skip these.
TaiyakiFish-shaped pastry filled with sweet red bean paste (anko), custard, or sweet potato — pressed hot to order. Crispy outside, soft and warm inside. One of Japan's most beloved street snacks. Kids who try one almost always want a second. ¥200–400.
Uni (sea urchin)Divisive but extraordinary. Adults: don't skip. Kids: warn them first.

The bright green color is extraordinary; other tourists will ask where you got it. Double-shot iced matcha latte + plain soft serve for kids. ¥600–900. Open Mon/Tue/Thu/Sat 8am–4pm. Closed Wed.

FRUITS MATCHA Mt.FUJIUpstairs above the market chaos — a café with a live pianist who plays while you eat. Japanese soufflé pancakes, matcha drinks, and double espresso. A completely unexpected oasis of calm literally on top of a fish market. Request songs from the pianist. ¥1,000–1,800 per person with food. Open daily 9am–3pm (Sat to 4pm). 2F above the market lanes.
Strategy: For a quick grab-and-wander: Matcha Stand Maruni or Turret Coffee. For a seated mid-market reset with kids who need 20 min off their feet: FRUITS MATCHA Mt.FUJI upstairs — calm, musical, genuinely surprising.
The tamagoyaki challenge: Each kid tries one new food. Tamagoyaki is the entry point — universally loved. From there, escalate to vegetable tempura or taiyaki. Whoever tries the most new things wins something at the next stop.
Knife shops: Stop into a professional knife shop — 4th-generation craftsmen, museum-quality blades. Many will demonstrate whetstone sharpening. Kids find the precision genuinely fascinating.
Animal sighting: Tsukiji market cats live in the lanes. Not guaranteed but kids who spot one feel like they won. Also: live shellfish and fish in vendor tanks that kids get transfixed by.
Professional knivesMasahiro, Misono, Tojiro. Quality chef's knife ¥8,000–25,000. The best kitchen knives you can buy in Japan.
Dried goodsKombu, bonito flakes, specialty soy sauces, wasabi. Light, packable, excellent gifts for anyone who cooks.

The Garden

A perfectly maintained feudal-era garden surrounded by skyscrapers and Tokyo Bay. Former duck-hunting grounds of the Tokugawa shogunate. Tidal ponds — water rises and falls with Tokyo Bay. A 300-year-old pine tree near the central pond predates the American Revolution. The contrast between this quiet and the surrounding skyline is one of the most Tokyo-specific visual experiences of the trip.
The 300-year-old pine: Have them put their hands on the bark. It was already 100 years old before the US existed. Massive, gnarled, supported by wooden props.
Tidal ponds & animals: The water rises and falls with ocean tides even though you're deep inside the city. Herons and egrets fish the shallows — excellent animal spotting. Wild ducks wander the paths with complete indifference to tourists.
The duck blinds: Traditional wooden duck-hunting blinds (kamo-ba) from the Tokugawa shogunate still stand near the ponds. Kids find the idea of samurai-era royalty hunting ducks here hilarious.
The Nakajima-no-Ochaya teahouse sits on a tiny island reached by two wooden bridges. ¥700/person: matcha + one wagashi sweet (seasonal mochi or bean-paste confection). One of the most peaceful 20-minute resets in all of Tokyo. Even matcha skeptics find this version — prepared properly in a beautiful setting — genuinely good.
Active kid split: Interested kids and adults do the tea ceremony; very active kids walk the nearby pond path supervised. Reconvene at the wooden bridge for the water bus.
From Hamarikyu Pier, board the Sumida River water bus (Suijō Bus) to Asakusa — 40 min up the river through old Tokyo. You pass under 14 historic bridges, see the Asahi Beer building with its famous golden "flame" on the roof, and arrive directly at Asakusa Pier, 2 min from Sensō-ji.
Logistics: Boats run every 40–60 min. ¥1,200 adult, ¥600 child (6–11), under 6 free. Buy at the pier. Total for family of 6: ~¥6,600. The single best transport decision of the trip.
Kids on the boat: Sit on the open deck if weather allows. Views of historic bridges are spectacular from the water. Bring Tsukiji snacks to eat on the boat.

Shopping
Tokyo's oldest temple (645 AD — nearly 1,400 years old), most visited (30M/year). Enter through Kaminarimon — the giant red lantern is 4m tall, 700kg. Walk Nakamise shopping street slowly to the main hall. At the hall: coin in box, two bows, two claps, make a wish, one bow. The giant incense burner (jokoro) — waft smoke toward yourself. Kids especially love this part.
Context for kids: This temple is 1,380 years old. The Roman Colosseum was already 600 years old when this was built. That usually lands.
Omikuji fortune slips: ¥100 each. Shake cylinder, match number, pull slip, tie bad luck to the rack. Do one per person. Compare at dinner.
Rickshaw rides: Depart from outside Kaminarimon. English-speaking operators pull you through historic Asakusa lanes. 30 min ~¥7,000–10,000 for 2. For 6 people, coordinate 3 rickshaws as a convoy. Kids remember this forever.
Kimono rental: Multiple shops nearby — ¥2,000–4,000 per person, hair included. Sizes for children as small as age 3. Kids in kimono walking the temple district is a memory-making move.
Name stamps in kanji: Shops on Nakamise engrave your name in Japanese katakana on a hanko stamp on the spot. ¥500–1,500. Every kid should get one.
Strawberry daifuku ⭐Whole fresh strawberry inside sweet red bean paste wrapped in soft mochi. Late March = peak strawberry season. Genuinely extraordinary. ¥300–450.
Imo Pippi — sweet potato brûlée ⭐The most viral street food in Asakusa right now. A slow-aged, slow-cooked Japanese sweet potato topped with custard cream and torched caramelized sugar — crème brûlée but make it a potato. The snap of the sugar crust into the silky custard and sweet potato is genuinely excellent. Also: baked sweet potato with honey butter (¥750), and a purple potato silk-thread ice cream (¥1,400, similar to matcha Mont Blanc construction). Near Sensō-ji. All year round. Follow the queue.
Daigakuimo — Oimoyasan KoshinA Meiji-era institution (est. 1884) on the main approach street: chunks of Japanese sweet potato deep-fried then tossed in glossy caramel honey syrup and scattered with black sesame. Crispy shell, pillowy interior. The original Tokyo sweet potato snack — university students ate these to save money in the 1920s depression, hence the name (daigaku = university). Sold by weight, freshly fried batches appear throughout the day. ¥500–800. Cash preferred.
Melonpan ice creamSweet bread roll sliced open, stuffed with soft-serve. Wildly popular, very photogenic. ¥500.
Ningyo-yakiBaked cakes shaped like lanterns and temple gates, filled with sweet red bean paste. The traditional Asakusa snack for 100+ years. ¥200 for 5.
AgemanjuDeep-fried sweet bun — custard version is the crowd pleaser. Hot and crispy. ¥200.
Coffee — specialty
February Coffee ⭐Asakusa's most beloved local café group — four branches within a 10-min walk of Sensō-ji, all serving the same deep-roast espresso that locals swear by. The original opened in 2012 and earned a spot in Tabelog's top 100 cafés; the Roastery branch lets you watch the pink roasting machine at work. Signature item: the kissaten-style pudding (set custard, caramel sauce, thick and wobbling) — order it with an espresso. Pelican bakery bread used for all food items; the thick-cut toast and cheese toast sets are exceptional. Open daily 8:15am–6:30pm. ¥700–1,000.
Fuglen AsakusaSecond location of the Norwegian café that sparked Tokyo's third-wave coffee scene. Beautiful two-floor converted space near the Sumida River, more spacious than the Tomigaya original, with outdoor benches. Same exceptional light-roast single-origin coffee, craft beer in the evenings, and their famous waffles with Norwegian brown cheese. Open daily. ¥700–900.
Matcha & Japanese sweets
Chacha Futatsume ⭐The viral matcha dessert spot right by Kaminarimon. Signature is the Matcha Mont Blanc Cone: layers of sponge cake, lemon marmalade, homemade bean paste, mascarpone cream, and ice cream, topped with matcha-chestnut paste piped in noodle form and finished with gold leaf. Staff hold the cone while squeezing the matcha topping so you can film it — very intentionally photogenic. Honest caveat: it's rich and large (easily split between two people), and some reviewers find it more matcha-chestnut paste than ice cream. If you want pure matcha ice cream, this isn't it. But as a family spectacle it's excellent. ¥1,200–1,600. Small seating area inside. Near Sensō-ji.
Kagurachaka puchi — "Universe's Thickest Matcha Latte"Near Asakusa Station on the Tsukuba Express. Famous for their matcha latte intensity scale: 8 levels, but you can only order level 8 (the Interdimensional Matcha Latte) after ordering level 7. Nishio matcha from Aichi Prefecture. Kids who like matcha will enjoy picking their level. ¥600–900. Open daily.
Matcha Cafe Chazen — make-your-own experienceAn experience-based matcha café near Sensō-ji where you make your own seasonal Japanese sweets (nerikiri — white bean paste molded into seasonal shapes) and then whisk your own matcha using authentic bowls and equipment in a traditional tea room setting. Photos in front of seasonal decorations included. Kids welcome, beginners welcome, staff guide you through it. ~30–45 min. Book ahead if possible. ¥1,500–2,000. Multilingual staff.
How to pick: Serious coffee + a great pudding → February Coffee. Lighter roast + beautiful space → Fuglen. Viral matcha dessert photo → Chacha Futatsume. Kids want to make something → Matcha Cafe Chazen. Matcha intensity game → Kagurachaka puchi.

Traditional toysWooden tops (koma), kendama, daruma dolls. More interesting and durable than plastic character goods. Good gifts for active kids.
Sanrio in AsakusaSeveral shops on and near Nakamise carry Hello Kitty merchandise. Not a flagship but good for browsing. Full Sanrio flagship (Ginza Gift Gate) is 20 min by subway.

The big walk: Asakusa → Hanayashiki → Kappabashi → Ueno Park → Yanaka
If energy allows, this is one of the great afternoon walks in Tokyo — a continuous thread of completely different neighborhoods, each with its own personality, connecting the ancient temple district to old-town Yanaka through the cultural heart of the city. Total distance ~4–5km, allow 90 min of walking plus stops. You'll pass through layers of Tokyo that most tourists never connect on foot.
Step 1 — Hanayashiki (~10 min from Sensō-ji)Japan's oldest amusement park (est. 1853) is a 5-min walk west of Sensō-ji through the Rokku entertainment district. See it even if you don't go in — the retro candy-colored façade is a genuine Tokyo time-warp. If the kids want to go in: ¥1,000 adults, ¥500 under-12, free under-7. Rides cost extra. About 19 attractions including Japan's oldest operating roller coaster (1953) and a traditional Japanese haunted house. Best for younger kids — scale is very small, nothing like Disney. 1–1.5 hrs if you go in.
Step 2 — Kappabashi Kitchen Town (~5 min from Hanayashiki)Walk 5 min northwest into the world's most concentrated street of professional kitchen supplies — 170 shops along an 800m strip selling everything imaginable for cooking and dining. Entered via a massive giant chef's head on the corner (unmissable). Look for the plastic food replica shops (Ganso Shokuhin Sample-ya is the most famous), where you can make your own fake tempura in a workshop (2,500 yen, reservations required). Also: world-class Japanese knives at Kama-Asa, beautiful ceramic bowls at Komatsuya, seasonal cookie molds at Yokoyama bakery supply. Most shops open 10am–6pm. Many closed Sundays — Monday is the better day for this, which is when you're there.
Step 3 — Ueno Park (~10–15 min walk from Kappabashi)Walk north through the Ueno area into the park's southern entrance. In late March during cherry blossom peak, the famous Sakura-dori Avenue is a 700m tunnel of 800 cherry trees overhead — one of the most dramatic sakura scenes in all of Japan. Shinobazu Pond at the park's south edge is quieter: cherry blossoms reflected in the water, a red pagoda on a small island, wild egrets and ducks. The Ueno Sakura Festival runs March 14–April 5, 2026 with food stalls, music, and illuminations until 10pm nightly. Walk through, don't rush.
Step 4 — Ameyoko Market (~5 min, at Ueno Station)A lively postwar street market under the elevated train tracks between Ueno and Okachimachi stations — originally a black market, now a dense, vibrant stretch of food stalls, dried goods, discount clothing, and snack vendors. Famous for cheap seafood, dried fruit and nut shops, and the general sensory chaos of old-town Tokyo commerce. A great 15-minute walk-through on the way north to Yanaka.
Step 5 — Yanaka (~20–25 min walk from Ueno)Continue north from Ueno through increasingly quiet residential streets, past small temples tucked between houses. You'll feel the city change as you walk — the noise drops, the streets narrow, wooden buildings appear. Enter from the Nippori Station side for the famous Sunset Steps (Yūyake-dandan) view over the rooftops before descending into Yanaka Ginza.
Time reality check: This full walk with stops takes 3.5–4 hours from Asakusa to Yanaka. If you've already spent 2 hours at Sensō-ji, plan to reach Yanaka around 4:30–5pm, giving you 1.5–2 hrs before dinner. That's enough. The walk is the experience — not a race.
Direct subway: Asakusa → Nippori
Asakusa Station (Ginza Line) → Ueno (1 stop, 4 min) → transfer to JR Yamanote Line → Nippori Station (2 stops, 5 min). Total: ~20 min including platform changes, ¥180/person. Or take a taxi directly from Asakusa to Nippori for ~¥1,000–1,400 for the group.
When to take the subway: If the kids are flagging after Sensō-ji and the temple visit took longer than expected, the subway is the right call. Yanaka itself is the destination — the walk is a bonus layer for families with energy to spare. Either way you end up in the same place.
Ueno-only option
Alternatively: Asakusa → Ueno (Ginza Line, 1 stop, 4 min, ¥180). Spend 30–45 min at Ueno Park for cherry blossoms and a quick Shinobazu Pond loop, then taxi or walk 20 min north to Yanaka. Gets you the best of Ueno without the full walk.
Hanayashiki — Japan's oldest amusement park (est. 1853)
Located a 5-min walk west of Sensō-ji, tucked behind Nakamise Street in the Rokku entertainment district. Built the same year Commodore Perry's ships arrived in Japan. Opened as a botanical garden, evolved into a zoo, became an amusement park in the post-war era. Now operated by Bandai Namco. About 19 attractions on a very compact footprint — the entire park is smaller than a single Disney "land."
The roller coasterJapan's oldest operating roller coaster, installed in 1953. Deliberately preserved in its Showa-era colors and look. Slow by modern standards, but the historical context makes it genuinely interesting. Kids who know what it is find it thrilling for different reasons than speed.
The haunted houseA traditional Japanese-style haunted house (obake yashiki) with a new attraction called "Curse of the Neck" as of July 2025. Dark, jump-scare style, very Japanese in its aesthetic. Kids who like haunted houses will love this. Kids who scare easily, avoid.
Festival stall gamesTraditional Japanese festival games (ennichi) — target shooting, ring toss, goldfish scooping. Very popular with tourists. The retro candy-colored aesthetic makes everything extremely photogenic. This is the most family-friendly section.
Practical infoEntry: ¥1,000 adult, ¥500 under-12, free under-7. Rides cost extra tokens (or buy a day pass). Open 10am–6pm. Worth entering for 60–90 min if kids have energy after the temple. Worth peering through the gate even if you skip it. The façade alone is worth a photo.
Verdict: Not a destination on its own — definitely not in the same conversation as Disneyland. But as a quick 60-min detour while you're already in Asakusa, the historical novelty is genuinely interesting and the retro atmosphere is like nothing else in Tokyo. The "reverse zoo" effect (you're inside, the rides are loose around you) is funny and charming.
Kappabashi Kitchen Town — the world's greatest kitchen street
A street you probably already know if you cook seriously: 170 shops along 800 meters between Ueno and Asakusa, selling everything used by Japan's professional restaurant industry. This is where Tokyo's chefs shop. Unlike most tourist streets, the goods here are genuinely professional-grade, and the prices reflect restaurant wholesale rather than souvenir markup. Open daily 10am–6pm; most shops closed Sundays — Monday March 23 is perfect.
Ganso Shokuhin Sample-yaThe famous plastic food replica shop — since 1932. The source of the hyper-realistic wax food models in restaurant windows throughout Japan. Kids can watch staff make them and buy keychains, magnets, and phone straps. Workshops to make your own fake tempura or ice cream sundae: ¥2,500, reservation required, held in Japanese but visual enough to follow. Kids aged 6+ welcome.
Kama-Asa knife shopOne of Tokyo's most respected knife stores, spread across two floors and two buildings. Japanese knives world-renowned for their precision — you can have your name engraved on the blade on the spot. Prices from ¥6,800 upward. The staff speak English and will let you handle the knives. If you're buying one knife to bring home from Japan, buy it here.
The giant chef's headA massive sculpture of a chef's head on the corner of Kappabashi and Asakusa-dori — the street's unofficial landmark and one of Tokyo's most delightfully strange photo opportunities. You genuinely cannot miss it. Great family photo.
What else to buyCeramic bowls (Komatsuya — stacked floor to ceiling, bargain prices), Japanese bento boxes (¥2,000–5,000 for beautiful lacquered versions), seasonal cookie molds in cherry blossom shapes (Yokoyama), chopsticks in every material imaginable, bamboo steamers, cast iron teapots, and hand-painted plates.

Tokyo's most beloved public park — 300 acres in the heart of the city, home to Japan's oldest zoo, a cluster of world-class museums, multiple shrines and temples, and 1,200 cherry trees that make it the most famous hanami spot in Tokyo. In late March during cherry blossom peak, the park transforms into a festival: Sakura-dori Avenue becomes a 700m tunnel of blossoms overhead, food stalls line the paths, 800 paper lanterns glow at night, and the city arrives in force for one of the great seasonal celebrations in Japan. Even a 30-min walk through is transformative.
Ueno Sakura Festival 2026: Running March 14–April 5. Illuminations nightly until 10pm along Shinobazu Pond. Food stalls, music performances on open-air stages. Free entry to the park.
Japan's oldest zoo (opened 1882) — Tokyo's most-visited zoo and home to the only giant pandas in Tokyo. Also: red pandas, gorillas, tigers, Asian elephants, polar bears, penguins, and 400+ species total. The panda viewing requires a separate timed entry ticket (book in advance online or grab one at the gate in the morning). Red pandas are a crowd favorite with kids — small, furry, chaotic little animals that move in ways that seem engineered to delight children.
Giant pandasTokyo's only pandas, housed in a purpose-built facility. Timed entry tickets required — book the first morning slot online before your trip if this is a priority. Pandas are most active in the morning.
Red pandas ⭐Far easier to see than the giant pandas, and genuinely more entertaining — they move constantly and seem engineered to delight children. The enclosure is well-positioned for viewing. A highlight for most families.

Immediately adjacent to Ueno Station, running under the JR elevated tracks between Ueno and Okachimachi — Ameyoko (short for "Ameya Yokocho," Candy Shop Lane) is a postwar black market that evolved into Tokyo's most chaotic and vibrant street market. Two long blocks packed with seafood vendors, dried goods shops, snack stalls, discount clothing, cosmetics, and the general energy of old-town Tokyo commerce at full volume.
What to eat hereFreshly grilled skewers, takoyaki (octopus balls), tuna toro rice bowls from the seafood vendors, dried mangoes and nuts, fresh fruit, and some of the most affordable seafood snacks in Tokyo. Budget ¥500–1,000 for a walk-through snack.
Long-established tea shopA famous matcha soft serve shop in Ameyoko serves rich, properly bitter matcha ice cream — one of the better versions in this area. Look for queues.
The experienceThis is old Tokyo at maximum sensory intensity — vendors calling out, narrow aisles, fish on ice, incense, dried goods hanging from storefronts. Walk through at a slow wander rather than with purpose. 15–20 min is enough. It's more atmosphere than destination.
Ueno Park in late March during cherry blossom peak is one of the top three hanami spots in all of Japan. Over 1,200 trees of 40 varieties bloom simultaneously — but the main event is Sakura-dori Avenue, a 700m central promenade with 800 cherry trees creating an overhead arch of blossoms. Walking under it feels like being inside a pink cloud. At the park's south end, Shinobazu Pond adds reflection — blossoms mirrored in still water, the red Benten-do temple pagoda framed in the distance, egrets fishing the shallows.
Sakura-dori AvenueThe main 700m promenade — walk it in both directions if time allows. This is the most famous sakura tunnel in Tokyo. In the afternoon it's packed, but the density of people is part of the festival atmosphere. Food stalls line both sides.
Shinobazu PondSouth end of the park. Quieter than the main avenue, more photogenic — blossoms reflected in the water, the red pagoda on a small island (Benten-do Temple), wild birds including egrets and cormorants. A calmer, more contemplative sakura experience.
Kiyomizu Kannon-do TempleA small 17th-century temple on a hill inside the park — a miniature version of Kyoto's famous Kiyomizu-dera, with a raised wooden balcony giving views over Shinobazu Pond and the cherry blossoms below. One of the park's most photogenic spots and largely overlooked by tourists.
Night illuminationsSakura Festival illuminations run until 10pm nightly through April 5. If you return to Ueno after dinner in Yanaka, the illuminated blossoms over Shinobazu Pond at night are a completely different and extraordinary experience — one of the best evening activities in Tokyo during cherry blossom season.

Yanaka is the Tokyo that survived. The March 1945 US firebombing raids that destroyed much of the city, and the 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake that flattened large swathes of it before that, largely spared this hillside neighborhood. The result is something genuinely rare in one of the world's most intensely developed cities: an intact pre-war residential district where the bones of old Edo are still visible.
Walking through Yanaka feels like a gradual decompression from modern Tokyo. The streets narrow. Buildings stay low — one or two floors, not twenty. Wooden machiya townhouses appear between the newer structures. Small family-run temples (over 70 of them in a tiny area) sit tucked between houses, their stone lanterns overgrown with moss. Cats sit on walls and gates with the proprietorial ease of longtime residents. And Yanaka Ginza — the main shopping street — is a 200m covered shotengai (arcade) that has been here since the postwar era, selling dried fish, fresh tofu, ceramic goods, handmade umbrellas, and cat-themed everything to a mix of locals and curious visitors.
The walk-in route from Nippori
Exit Nippori Station and head west along the top of the ridge. Almost immediately you hit the famous Sunset Steps (Yūyake-dandan) — a broad staircase descending into Yanaka Ginza, named for the view of Tokyo's rooftops at dusk that was once unobstructed all the way to Mount Fuji. At the top of the steps, turn and look south over the low rooftops before you descend. This is one of the great quiet views in Tokyo. Then walk down into the shopping street and go wherever looks interesting.
The key to Yanaka: Don't rush it and don't follow a list. The point of Yanaka is wandering — turning down a small lane because a cat is sitting on a gate, finding a tiny temple you didn't expect, stumbling into a pottery workshop with the door open. The more you try to "do" Yanaka efficiently, the more you miss it.
The neighborhood experience itself is the activity here — but here are the specific things to look for and do:
Yanaka CemeteryDirectly south of the Sunset Steps — one of Tokyo's largest cemeteries but atmospheric rather than grim, especially in late March when the 500+ cherry trees within the grounds are in full bloom. Wide tree-lined paths, stone lanterns, old cherry trees bending over graves. One of the most beautiful hanami spots in the entire Yanaka-Ueno area, and almost entirely missed by tourists because they hear "cemetery" and skip it.
The last shogun's graveTokugawa Yoshinobu — the last samurai ruler of Japan, who peacefully surrendered power in 1868 ending 250 years of shogunate rule — is buried in Yanaka Cemetery. His grave is remarkably modest for someone who changed the course of Japanese history. Find it on Google Maps beforehand and let kids navigate to it. The story of the samurai era ending here and what came next is genuinely gripping.
Cat spotting competitionYanaka is famous across Tokyo for its free-roaming neighborhood cats — they sit on walls, sleep in doorways, claim entire sections of alleyway. They are part of the neighborhood's character. Whoever spots the most cats in 30 minutes wins dessert. Most kids find 4–6 real cats in an hour of wandering. Many of the shops also sell cat-themed goods that reinforce the theme.
Temple countingOver 70 temples in a tiny area — most of them tiny, family-run, and completely unvisited by tourists. Challenge kids to find 5 different temple gates (torii or sanmon) in the winding side streets. The gates range from grand stone structures to small wooden ones half-hidden behind bamboo. This turns the wandering into a game.
SCAI The BathhouseA contemporary art gallery operating inside a perfectly preserved traditional bathhouse (sento) from the Edo period — the contrast of cutting-edge contemporary art (Ai Weiwei, Anish Kapoor have all shown here) inside a building designed for communal bathing is one of the great Tokyo surprises. Free or low admission depending on exhibition. Worth a 15-min peek.
Yanaka Coffee-ten — a small roastery on Yanaka Ginza selling beans from around the world. Excellent iced coffee to go; the house blend is fruity and mellow. Walk and sip — no seating inside, just a takeaway window essentially.
Guru Guru Gelato — right on Yanaka Ginza. Excellent matcha and melon gelato, gluten-free crepes, and a cozy wood interior with actual seating. The matcha float is the local favorite. Kids and adults both love this stop. One of the few places in Yanaka where you can sit down with everyone.
The old lady's stall: Multiple reviewers independently describe an older woman on a side stall selling salted snacks — wasabi peas, sembei rice crackers. Cash only. She can't be found on any map and isn't listed anywhere officially, but people seek her out specifically. The snacks are reportedly exceptional and she's described as deeply lovely. This is the kind of encounter Yanaka is built for.
Also nearby: Several small retro cafés in Yanaka serve drip coffee surrounded by vintage furniture and vinyl records — the quintessential Yanaka café experience is unhurried, local, slightly time-warped. Ask Google Maps for "Yanaka café" and walk toward whichever looks most appealing from the outside.
Yanaka Ginza shops~70 shops along the 200m covered arcade: dried fish, fresh-pressed tofu, handmade ceramics, bamboo goods, traditional sweets, local sake, craft goods. Prices are local — not tourist markup. This is where the neighborhood shops, not where tourists come to buy souvenirs, though you can buy great souvenirs here.
Cat goods shopsMultiple shops dedicated entirely to cat-themed ceramics, textiles, cards, jewelry, and illustrated goods. Yanaka's unofficial brand. The best shops have beautiful hand-painted pieces rather than mass-produced character goods. Look for the ones where someone is clearly making the items themselves.
Artisan workshopsSeveral small workshops operate traditional crafts — pottery, woodworking, lacquerware, textile dyeing. Some have doors open during working hours and you can watch. Pieces for sale are genuinely one-of-a-kind. Nothing here has a SKU number or a brand.
Yanaka Beer HallA converted historic building operating as a neighborhood craft beer pub. Very local, very casual, extremely good. The perfect adults-only end-of-day stop while kids eat nearby. No tourist atmosphere whatsoever.
Yanaka is not a shopping destinationUnlike Harajuku or Nakamise, Yanaka isn't a place you go to buy things specifically. It's a place you go to wander, and sometimes you buy something small along the way. The best things you take home from Yanaka are photos, memories of the cats, and the feeling of having found a Tokyo that most people don't see.

The Imperial Palace — home of Japan's Emperor — sits on the former site of Edo Castle in the center of Tokyo, surrounded by a wide moat and stone walls that have stood for 350+ years. The actual palace interior is closed to the public almost year-round, but the exterior view from the plaza and the East Gardens (open to the public free of charge) give you a genuinely impressive slice of both feudal and modern Japan. In late March, cherry blossoms line the moat and bloom throughout the gardens — the combination of stone castle walls, water reflections, and sakura is quintessentially Japanese and completely unlike anything else in Tokyo.
The two things everyone comes for
Nijubashi (Double Bridge) view ⭐The most iconic image of the Imperial Palace — two stone bridges arching over the moat in front of the Fushimi-yagura watchtower. Visible from the plaza outside the main gate without entering anywhere. This is the photo op. It takes about 10 minutes to walk from the taxi drop-off on the palace perimeter to this view. Free, no tickets, always accessible.
Chidorigafuchi Moat — cherry blossom walkThe northwestern moat of the palace, lined with cherry trees that in late March create one of the most spectacular hanami corridors in Tokyo. A wide, calm walkway along the water's edge with blossoms overhead and reflected in the moat below. 10-min walk from the main plaza. In a different category from any other cherry blossom spot you'll see — the scale and the historical setting together are extraordinary.
With 75 minutes (12:45–2:00pm), here's the most efficient route to see the essential things without rushing:
12:45
Taxi drop-off at Kokyo Gaien plaza (the large public square in front of the main gate). Walk toward the moat — Nijubashi bridge view is right in front of you. Photos. 10 min.
1:00
Walk to Ote-mon Gate (5 min from the plaza) — the main entrance to the East Gardens. Free entry, no ticket, hand in a numbered card at the gate and return it on exit.
1:05
Inside East Gardens: Wide lawns where Edo Castle's main keep once stood — nothing above ground now but the stone foundation platform, which you can climb. The emptiness is intentional and eerie in a good way. Cherry trees throughout at late-March peak.
1:25
Ninomaru Garden (a 5-min stroll from the keep platform) — designed in the Edo period, restored 1968. Traditional Japanese strolling garden with irises, stone paths, a small pond. Genuinely beautiful for 10 min of quiet wandering.
1:40
Exit Ote-mon Gate → walk or taxi south. If energy allows: 10-min walk west toward Chidorigafuchi for the moat cherry blossom walk. If tight on time, taxi directly to the hotel from here.
2:00
⚠️ Hard exit. In taxi toward ANA InterContinental (15–20 min, ¥1,200–1,800). Collect bags. Depart hotel at 3:00pm for Shinagawa.
Getting there from Azabudai HillsTaxi ~15 min, ¥1,200–1,500. Tell driver "Kokyo Gaien" (皇居外苑) — the public plaza in front of the main palace gate. This drops you directly in front of the Nijubashi view.
East Gardens hours on March 25Open 9:00am–5:00pm. Wednesday is open (closed Mondays and Fridays — you're safe on Wednesday). Free entry. No reservation needed. Hand in a numbered card at Ote-mon gate, return it on exit. Opens to the public year-round.

Collect pre-stored bags (takes 5 min with hotel staff's help if you alert them in advance). Then second taxi to Shinagawa: ~20–25 min, ¥2,000–2,500. Leave hotel by 3:00pm. Arrive Shinagawa ~3:20pm. Shinkansen gates by 3:30pm — 37 min before departure.
Skip Chidorigafuchi if behind scheduleThe moat walk is beautiful but it adds 20+ min. If you exit the Gardens at 1:40pm instead of 1:30pm, skip Chidorigafuchi and go straight to the hotel. The Nijubashi view and the East Gardens alone are worth the stop.
Set a hard alarm at 2:00pm. The Palace is the kind of place you wander longer than planned. The 2:00pm exit is non-negotiable given the train.
The Imperial Palace grounds are one of the most geographically significant and historically layered sites in all of Japan — this is where 250 years of samurai rule was administered, where the last shogun surrendered power to Emperor Meiji in 1868, and where Japan's modern transformation began. That doesn't look like much on the ground (it's mostly lawn and walls), but the context changes what you're seeing.
The stone platform where the castle keep stoodYou can climb the original stone foundation of the main Edo Castle keep — the tower itself burned down in 1657 and was never rebuilt. Standing on top of it with the Tokyo skyline visible in every direction: this was the most powerful military position in Japan for 250 years, controlled by the Tokugawa shoguns your kids heard about in Yanaka. Now you're standing on it.
"This is where the samurai lived"Kids who learned about the last shogun in Yanaka Cemetery (Day 2) will make the connection — the same Tokugawa family whose last member is buried in Yanaka ran Japan from this location. This is the end of that story. Worth making explicit.
The moatThe stone walls and wide moat were built to be militarily impenetrable — 17th-century Tokyo equivalent of a fortress. Kids who get into the engineering (how do you build that? how deep is it? what was it like to attack this?) will find it engaging. The answer to all three: very deliberately, very deep, and essentially impossible.

Inside Shinagawa
Large, clearly signed for Shinkansen. Follow signs. Seats should already be reserved — essential for a family of 6. JR Ticket Office (Midori no Madoguchi) can help if needed. Allow 20 min from taxi drop-off to platform.
Ekiben — train bento boxes
Sold in shops before the Shinkansen gates — some of the best train meals in the world. Get one per person plus drinks from the 7-Eleven inside the station.
Ekiben for kids: Look for character bento boxes — often Shinkansen or Pokémon themed, cute, tasty, and a souvenir box in itself. The Makunouchi bento (assorted rice, fish, tamagoyaki, pickles) is the classic for anyone.
Hikari or Nozomi Shinkansen to Kyoto: approximately 2 hours 15 minutes. Smooth, fast, impressively quiet. One of the great engineering achievements in the world — runs to the second, reaches 320 km/h.
Mt. Fuji — don't miss: Sit on the RIGHT side of the train (seats A/B facing forward). Fuji appears ~30–45 min into the journey. Departing at 4:07pm → look right at 4:40–4:50pm. Visible for about 5 minutes. Set a phone alarm. Cameras ready. Late March has a good chance of a clear view.
Arriving in Kyoto (~6:20pm): Kyoto Station is enormous and spectacular. Taxis queue directly outside. Allow 15–30 min for the taxi queue on a busy evening.
Kids love the Shinkansen: Tell them the speed before boarding (320 km/h). They'll spend the first 20 min watching the landscape blur past the windows. The train is an experience worth paying attention to.
Activity on the ride: Each person names one thing from Tokyo they'd want to bring back or do again. Compare answers. The 2-hour window is a perfect reflection space between Tokyo and Kyoto.
